PART ONE
Determining the Optimal Inventory Level for Replenishment
================================================================================

You've started to examine the various analysis and calculations involved in inventory optimization using methods like base-stock policy. In this part of the Unit 2 Assignment, you will determine the optimal inventory needed for replenishment for the provided scenario and dataset.

Scenario
--------
Cosmo sells a wide range of electronic products to retailers, primarily through third-party distributors, in the US. The company produces its leading product, the HD3040 TV set, at its major factory in Kansas City, ships the product to its regional distribution centers (RDCs), from which orders from the distributors' distribution centers (DCs) are filled.

Daily demand for HD3040 at the DCs of three New York state distributors, have means and standard deviations given below. All three DCs are supplied by the RDC of Cosmo, located in Brooklyn, NY.

Albany            120           60
Brooklyn          180           80
Syracuse          80            60

You may assume the following facts in your analysis:
	•The DC and RDC operate 7 days a week, 52 weeks per year.
	•The DCs place orders to the RDC once a week, and have a service level target of 98%.
	•The lead time from the RDC to the DCs have means and standard deviations given below.

This lead time includes the time to process orders, retrieve stock form the warehouse of the RDC, some handling and packaging the shipment, loading, waiting for the trucks to consolidate a full truckload of shipments (possibly of other products to other customers), the actual transit time, making some drop-offs of other products to some locations along the way, unloading and the final receipt and put away by the DC.

DC Location    Mean Lead Time (Days)    Std Dev of Lead Time (Days)
Albany         10                       2

Transportation Segment         Current Rate Per Unit
Plant to Brooklyn RDC          $3.00

	•The Brooklyn RDC orders the SKU weekly from the Kansas City plant. These orders are filled from finished goods inventory at the plant.
	•The lead time from the plant to the Brooklyn RDC is 10 days, with a standard deviation of 2 days. Again, this lead time is composed of similar waiting, handling, and transit time as the lead time from the RDC to the DC. The RDC has a service level target of 98%.

3. A recent audit of the Brooklyn RDC over the past year's performance revealed that the RDC's average safety stock had been at 7 days of supply (meaning the safety stock can last for 7 days of demand), while an average fill level of 96% was achieved.

Do you think that the RDC's inventory management has been efficient? Why?

Answer:

No, it is not efficient. Seven days of supply at the RDC equals 2,660 units (7 * 380), which implies a service level around 99.85%, yet the audit reports only 96% fill rate. That means they are holding more inventory than the scientific policy requires while delivering worse service. Under the base-stock policy, the RDC needs only 1,847 units to hit 98% service, so the current approach is both more expensive and less effective.

4. What is the magnitude of improvement that you think can be made as a result of using scientific inventory methods (e.g., what service improvement could be achieved with the same inventory levels, or how much inventory could be reduced without any service degradation)?

Answer:

With 2,660 units of safety stock (current 7-day supply), the scientific method would achieve approximately 99.85% service level instead of the current 96%. Alternatively, to maintain the same 96% service level scientifically, only 1,574 units of safety stock would be required (z = 1.751 for 96%), representing a reduction of 1,086 units (41% reduction) without any service degradation.

The potential annual savings from reducing safety stock by 1,086 units:
= 1,086 units × $800/unit × 25% holding rate = $217,200 per year
